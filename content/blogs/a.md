Your water heater works tirelessly behind the scenes, providing hot showers, clean dishes, and warm laundry water. But like any hardworking appliance, it has a limited lifespan. Most tank water heaters last 8–12 years, while tankless models can go 15–20 years with proper maintenance. However, even the best units show warning signs before they fail completely.

Recognizing these symptoms early can save you from a cold shower, water damage, and an emergency replacement bill. Here are the five most common signs that your water heater is nearing the end of its life.

## 1. Rusty or Discolored Water

If you turn on the hot water tap and see brown, reddish, or cloudy water, that's a red flag. Rust-colored water usually means the inside of your water heater tank is corroding. While a corroded anode rod can be replaced, if the tank itself is rusting, it's only a matter of time before a leak develops. Test this by running only the cold water - if it runs clear, the issue is likely your water heater.

## 2. Strange Noises: Rumbling, Popping, or Banging

Over time, sediment builds up at the bottom of the tank. When the burner heats the water, the sediment traps heat, causing water to boil and create popping or rumbling sounds. While a flush can remove some sediment, excessive noise indicates heavy buildup that reduces efficiency and can lead to overheating and tank failure.

## 3. Leaks or Moisture Around the Base

A small puddle near your water heater is never normal. Leaks can come from a loose valve or a cracked tank. If the leak is coming from the tank itself and not a fitting, replacement is usually the only solution. Even a slow leak can cause significant water damage over time.

## 4. Inconsistent Water Temperature or No Hot Water

Does your shower go from hot to cold without warning? Or is the water just lukewarm even with the thermostat turned up? This could be a failing heating element in electric units or a broken dip tube. If the unit is older, repair may not be cost-effective compared to replacement.

## 5. Age Over 10–12 Years

Even if it seems to be working fine, a water heater beyond its expected lifespan is a risk. Newer models are also much more energy-efficient, so replacing an aging unit can lower your utility bills and give you peace of mind.
